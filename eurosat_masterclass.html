Understanding EuroSAT

Why satellite imagery is fundamentally different from everything you've done before

🛰️ What is EuroSAT?

EuroSAT is a benchmark dataset created in 2019 by Helber et al. from Darmstadt University and the European Space Agency. It uses imagery from the Sentinel-2 satellite — part of the European Copernicus Earth Observation programme.

Total Images
27,000
Labeled patches
Image Size
64×64
RGB pixels
Resolution
10m/px
Real Earth per pixel

Each image covers 640m × 640m of real Earth surface. The dataset has 10 land-use classes: AnnualCrop, Forest, HerbaceousVegetation, Highway, Industrial, Pasture, PermanentCrop, Residential, River, SeaLake.

💡
Why EuroSAT Was Created
Before EuroSAT, there was no clean, large-scale, publicly available benchmark for satellite image classification using CNNs. EuroSAT democratized remote sensing research and proved that modern transfer learning could work on satellite imagery.
🌍 The Three Worlds of Vision Data

You have now worked in three distinct visual worlds. Understanding how they differ is fundamental to making correct architectural decisions.

ProjectViewpointSubjectWhat the CNN Learns
CIFAR-10Eye-level, perspectiveSingle object fills frameObject shapes, contours, depth cues
Ant vs. BeeMacro close-upSingle insect, body morphologyBody parts, wing structure, color patterns
EuroSATOrbital, 800km, nadirNo single subject — the PATCH is the classSpatial texture patterns, statistical distributions
🧠
The Critical Insight
EuroSAT is a texture classification problem, not an object recognition problem. The model does not find a "thing" and classify it — it classifies the statistical texture of the entire patch. This changes everything about how you think about feature learning.
⚡ 5 Fundamental Differences from CIFAR-10 / Ant vs. Bee

Difference 1 — No "Subject" in the Frame

In Ant vs. Bee, there was a central subject. In EuroSAT, there is no subject. A "Forest" image is not a picture of a tree — it is thousands of trees viewed from so far away that individual trees are 2-3 pixels wide. Every pixel contributes equal information. There is no background to crop away.

Difference 2 — Orthographic (Top-Down) Viewpoint

Satellite imagery is perfectly nadir (straight-down). There is zero perspective distortion, no depth cues, no shadow casting from objects toward the viewer. A building appears as a perfect rectangle of pixels. This completely invalidates RandomPerspective augmentation.

Difference 3 — Severe Domain Shift from ImageNet

ImageNet: 1.2M photographs of everyday objects, human-scale, eye-level, standard camera. Sentinel-2: orbital satellite sensor, orthographic, atmospheric correction applied. The statistical distributions of both datasets are dramatically different in mean and variance.

Difference 4 — Different Spectral Information

Sentinel-2 has 13 spectral bands including Near-Infrared (NIR) and Short-Wave Infrared (SWIR), invisible to human eyes. Even in the RGB version, colors carry different information — vegetation reflects NIR that gets blended in, water appears near-black.

Difference 5 — Inherently Ambiguous Class Boundaries

Ant vs. Bee has clean biological boundaries. EuroSAT's land-use classes have human-assigned, fuzzy boundaries. What exactly separates HerbaceousVegetation from Pasture? AnnualCrop from PermanentCrop? These ambiguities create systematic confusion pairs in the model.

⚠️
Highway Width Reality Check
A 2-lane highway is ~7.5 meters wide. Each pixel = 10 meters. Therefore: 7.5 ÷ 10 = 0.75 pixels. The road itself is sub-pixel. The model cannot detect Highway by looking at the road — it learns the surrounding ecosystem (markings, guardrails, shoulders, surrounding context).
↔️ Domain Shift — Two Components

Domain shift has two distinct dimensions. Both are larger between ImageNet→EuroSAT than ImageNet→Ant/Bee.

Component 1: Input Distribution Shift (Covariate Shift)

The statistical distribution of pixel values P(X) is different. ImageNet and Ant/Bee are both human-scale photography with natural lighting and perspective — statistically close. EuroSAT has orthographic geometry, satellite sensor calibration, and atmospheric effects — statistically far from ImageNet.

Component 2: Label Space Shift

ImageNet → Ant/Bee: still classifying biological organisms — a subset of ImageNet concepts. ImageNet → EuroSAT: no class overlap whatsoever. No ImageNet class maps to "AnnualCrop" or "SeaLake."

🔬 Why Does ImageNet Transfer Work on Satellite Data?

The original EuroSAT paper showed ResNet50 pre-trained on ImageNet achieves 98.6% accuracy on satellite imagery — despite massive domain shift. Why?

Two reasons work together:

Reason 1 — Background Features in ImageNet: Many ImageNet photos have backgrounds of grass, roads, water, sky, and foliage. These backgrounds contain satellite-relevant textures. The model has seen grass texture and road texture, just from a different angle and scale.

Reason 2 — Hierarchical Feature Universality (The Deep Reason):

📊 Layer Specificity Spectrum

This is the most important mental model in transfer learning. Every layer in a CNN learns features of different abstraction and domain-specificity.

Conv1, Conv2
Edges, color gradients, Gabor filters, blobs. Physics of light — same in ANY image.
🟢 Universal (100%)
Conv3, Conv4
Textures, local patterns (fur, fabric, grain). Partially overlap with satellite textures.
🟡 Semi-Universal (50%)
Conv5+
High-level semantics (dog ears, car wheels, faces). Domain-specific. NOT transferable.
🔴 Domain-Specific (10%)
FC Layers
Class decisions (logit for "dog"). Completely task-specific. Always replace.
🔴 Replace Always (0%)
✅
The Transfer Learning Truth
We do not transfer "knowledge about dogs." We transfer universal visual primitives — mathematical representations of edges and textures that are useful for ANY image domain, including satellites.
⚙️ The Three Fine-Tuning Strategies

This is the industry-standard framework for deciding how much of a pre-trained model to re-train.

StrategyWhat Gets FrozenWhen to UseEuroSAT?
Feature Extraction ALL conv layers Tiny dataset + similar domain (Ant vs. Bee) ❌ Too aggressive
Partial Fine-Tuning First N layers only (Conv1-2) Medium domain shift, medium data ✅ Our strategy
Full Fine-Tuning Nothing frozen Large dataset + very different domain ⚠️ Risky without care
Progressive Unfreezing Unfreeze layer by layer per epoch Maximum control, research-grade 🔬 Advanced option
🎯 Decision for EuroSAT
Freeze Conv1-Conv2 (universal features — no benefit in retraining). Fine-tune Conv3-Conv5 (domain-specific textures must adapt). Replace + train FC layer (new 10-class task).
📉 Discriminative Learning Rates

When fine-tuning, you cannot use the same learning rate for all layers. Each layer group requires a different learning rate based on how much it needs to change.

# Discriminative Learning Rate Setup for EuroSAT
optimizer = torch.optim.Adam([
    {'params': model.layer3.parameters(), 'lr': 1e-4},  # Pre-trained, gentle nudge
    {'params': model.layer4.parameters(), 'lr': 1e-4},  # Pre-trained, gentle nudge
    {'params': model.fc.parameters(),     'lr': 1e-3},  # New layer, learn aggressively
], lr=1e-4)  # default for any unlisted param group
Layer GroupStatusLearning RateWhy
Conv1-Conv2Frozenlr = 0Universal features — perfect already
Conv3-Conv5 (layer3, layer4)Fine-tuninglr = 1e-4Gentle adaptation without destroying useful features
FC (new layer)Training from scratchlr = 1e-3Randomly initialized — needs to learn fast
💥 Catastrophic Forgetting

If you apply a large learning rate (e.g., 1e-3) to layers that were carefully trained on 1.2M ImageNet images, the gradient updates are so large that the weights violently overwrite the existing feature representations.

🚨
What Catastrophic Forgetting Looks Like
Conv3-5 filters that had learned to detect textures, patterns, and edges are destroyed and replaced with random noise. The model essentially forgets everything from ImageNet. Training becomes unstable, loss spikes, and the model performs worse than pure feature extraction.
✅
The Fix
Use small learning rate (1e-4 or lower) for pre-trained layers. This "gently nudges" the weights toward satellite-specific features without destroying the valuable ImageNet representations already encoded in those weights.

Why Green Is Lower Than Expected

Counter-intuitive finding: Despite 4/10 classes being vegetation, EuroSAT's Green channel mean (0.380) is LOWER than ImageNet's (0.456). Three reasons:

1. Satellite sensors measure raw radiance — no saturation boost, no aesthetic enhancement like consumer cameras. All values are more muted.

2. Non-vegetation classes pull the mean down — SeaLake (near-black), River (dark), Highway (grey), Industrial (grey) all have low G values and they outnumber vegetation classes.

3. Atmospheric absorption — The atmosphere absorbs red light (Rayleigh scattering), suppressing the Red channel most severely (0.344, the biggest drop).

Why Low Std Matters for Domain Shift

🧠
The Std Gap Quantifies Domain Shift
EuroSAT's Green Std (0.137) is 39% lower than ImageNet's (0.224). EuroSAT's Blue Std (0.116) is 48% lower. This means EuroSAT images are statistically "monotone" — dominated by one color per class. ImageNet has enormous color variety. ResNet18's BatchNorm layers were calibrated for ImageNet's high-variance distributions. This is why fine-tuning BatchNorm layers matters even during partial fine-tuning.
🔄 Augmentation Strategy — Final Decision

The golden rule: only apply augmentations that produce images that could realistically have been captured by your actual sensor.

✅
RandomHorizontalFlip(p=0.5)
Valid: Satellite orbital pass direction varies. A flipped forest is still a valid satellite capture.
✅
RandomVerticalFlip(p=0.5)
Valid: Same rotational symmetry reasoning. Earth has no "right-side-up" from orbit.
✅
RandomRotation(degrees=90)
KING of remote sensing augmentation. The most important one. Always use it.
✅
ColorJitter(brightness, contrast, saturation, hue)
Breaks color shortcuts. Forces model to learn texture/pattern instead of just green=vegetation.
❌
RandomPerspective
NEVER. Creates perspective-distorted images that no satellite ever captures. Destroys the orthographic geometry. Will hurt performance.
❌
CenterCrop (as augmentation)
In EuroSAT, every pixel is signal — there is no "background" to eliminate. Cropping removes useful spatial information.

🚨
The Test Set is Sacred — Never Touch It During Training
If you check test accuracy every epoch and use it to decide when to stop, you have leaked the test set into training. Your final reported accuracy is dishonest — it reflects test-set overfitting. The test set is evaluated ONCE at the very end after all hyperparameter decisions are made.

Why Stratification is Mandatory

from sklearn.model_selection import train_test_split

all_labels = np.array([full_dataset[i][1] for i in range(len(full_dataset))])
all_indices = np.arange(len(full_dataset))

# Step 1: Separate test set (15%)
train_val_idx, test_idx = train_test_split(
    all_indices, test_size=0.15,
    stratify=all_labels,   # ← Each class proportional in test set
    random_state=42
)

# Step 2: Separate validation (15% of total ≈ 17.6% of train_val)
train_idx, val_idx = train_test_split(
    train_val_idx, test_size=0.176,
    stratify=all_labels[train_val_idx],
    random_state=42
)
⚖️ Normalization — The Mathematics and the Decision

The Normalization Pipeline

Normalization Formula
$$ x_{\text{normalized}} = \frac{x - \mu}{\sigma} $$

Raw pixel [0,255] → ToTensor() divides by 255 → range [0.0, 1.0]

After Normalize(ImageNet stats) → range ≈ [-2.1, +2.2]

⚠️
What Happens If You Forget Normalize()
Raw uint8 pixels (0-255) enter ResNet18. Expected range: [-2.1, 2.2]. Activations explode ~100× larger than expected → BatchNorm fails → ReLU saturates → deeper layers receive garbage → model output is random → you train for 10 epochs wondering why accuracy is stuck at 10% → you find the missing Normalize() at 2am.
🎯 Final Normalization Decision
Use ImageNet stats (mean=[0.485, 0.456, 0.406], std=[0.229, 0.224, 0.225]) for partial fine-tuning. The frozen Conv1-Conv2 layers expect inputs in ImageNet-normalized range. Using EuroSAT-specific stats with frozen layers would disrupt their activation patterns.

Baseline CNN Architecture

Building a custom 3-block CNN from scratch to establish a scientific baseline

🏗️ Designing the Custom CNN

To measure the true impact of Transfer Learning, we first need a baseline. Our baseline is a 3-block CNN built from scratch. It is deliberately simple to show how a model performs without pre-trained knowledge.

Why Not 8 Layers?

💡
The Purpose of a Baseline
A baseline shouldn't try to get the highest accuracy possible. If a custom 8-layer network takes hours to train and scores 85%, and Transfer Learning scores 94%, the story is weak. A 3-layer baseline establishes a fast, honest minimum capability (e.g., ~65%), proving Transfer Learning is truly transformative.

Architectural Decisions

Design ChoiceImplementationReasoning
Initial Conv Layer7×7, stride=2Quickly reduces large 224×224 input spatial dimensions while capturing broad patterns efficiently (same strategy as ResNet).
Deep Conv Layers3×3, stride=1Finer texture learning. Three 3×3 convs have the same receptive field as one 7×7 but fewer parameters and more non-linearities.
NormalizationBatchNorm2dApplied after Conv, before ReLU. Normalizes the full symmetric distribution before ReLU clips negative values.
Spatial ReductionMaxPool(2,2)Halves spatial dimensions at each block.
RegularizationDropout(0.5)Prevents severe overfitting on a model with 12.9M random parameters learning from only 18,900 images.

Pattern 1 — The Epoch 19 Crash

Epoch 19 saw Val Loss spike from 0.604 → 0.836 and accuracy crash 9%. The model fully recovered in Epoch 20. This is stochastic training noise — Dropout randomly killed specific neurons, ColorJitter produced an unusually hard validation distribution, and the model was near the edge of its learning capacity.

🛡️
Why "Save Best Model" is Non-Negotiable
Without saving the best checkpoint at Epoch 17, the final model after Epoch 19 would be 68.26% — 9.5% worse. This is why we always track best_val_f1 and checkpoint immediately. Never use the last epoch as your final model.

Pattern 2 — LR Scheduler Never Triggered

The scheduler (patience=3, factor=0.5) needs 3 consecutive epochs of non-improving val_loss. Because the model kept improving, the scheduler never fired. This tells you the model was still productively learning through epoch 20. With 30 epochs, it would likely reach 79–81% before plateauing.

The 5 Critical Confusion Pairs

🔴
#1 — River ↔ Highway (100 + 39 = 139 total errors) — WORST PAIR
Bidirectional confusion. The baseline learned "thin linear feature" as its river detector. It cannot distinguish River (organic curves, dark blue-black, fuzzy edges) from Highway (straight line, grey-white, sharp edges). Without ImageNet pre-trained oriented edge detectors, the model never learned color + edge regularity as the true discriminating feature.
🔴
#2 — Pasture → River (69 errors) — THE BIG SURPRISE
Nobody predicted this. Green flat fields classified as rivers 69 times. Reason: Many European pastures have irrigation channels and drainage ditches — thin dark water lines through the green field. The model's "any dark thin line" detector misfires on these drainage features. Phase 5 Grad-CAM will literally show the model staring at the drainage ditch and calling it a river.
🟡
#3 — HerbaceousVegetation → PermanentCrop (66 errors)
Predicted in Phase 1. Both are green, vegetated, without strong structure. Baseline cannot separate them because it never learned the subtle texture difference: HerbVeg is smooth and flat, PermanentCrop has faint orchard row patterns.

Transfer Learning — ResNet18

Partial fine-tuning with discriminative LR: 97.70% Macro-F1 achieved

🏗️ Strategy & Architecture

We load a ResNet18 pre-trained on ImageNet (1.2M natural photos, 1000 classes) and adapt it to EuroSAT's satellite imagery using partial fine-tuning — our chosen strategy from Phase 1 theory.

Freezing Strategy

conv1 + bn1
7×7 initial feature extractor — edge detectors, color blobs. Completely universal across all visual domains.
🔒 FROZEN
layer1 (×2 blocks)
64-channel residual blocks — corners, gradients, simple textures. Still highly universal.
🔒 FROZEN
layer2 (×2 blocks)
128-channel — complex textures, edges in context. Semi-universal; still valuable from ImageNet.
🔒 FROZEN
layer3 (×2 blocks)
256-channel — object parts, semantic regions. Retrained with small LR to adapt to satellite imagery.
✅ TRAIN (1e-4)
layer4 (×2 blocks)
512-channel — high-level semantic features. Retrained with small LR for domain-specific patterns.
✅ TRAIN (1e-4)
fc (new head)
512 → 10. Completely replaced — randomly initialized, trained from scratch at larger LR.
✅ TRAIN (1e-3)

Discriminative Learning Rates

The key innovation is using different learning rates for different parameter groups. This prevents Catastrophic Forgetting of the valuable ImageNet representations in the frozen layers while allowing meaningful adaptation in the trainable layers.

Explainable AI — Grad-CAM

Visualizing what ResNet18 actually looks at to make predictions.

🧠 The "Why" Behind the Errors

We achieved 97.7% accuracy, but 11 River images were still classified as Highway. To understand why, we implemented Grad-CAM on ResNet18's final convolutional layer (`layer4`). Grad-CAM computes the gradients of the target class score with respect to the feature maps, showing us exactly which pixels strongly influenced the model's decision.

💡
What the Model Actually Sees
When you run Grad-CAM on a correctly classified River image, the heatmap does not activate on the flat water surface. It activates entirely on the river banks (the boundary between water and land).
ClassWhat Grad-CAM HighlightsWhy?
RiverThe banks / water-land boundaryHigh contrast edge.
HighwayThe road edges / shoulder boundaryParallel linear edges.
Pasture / HerbVegScattered homogeneouslyTexture-based classification, no sharp edges.
🔍 The ImageNet Edge Bias

Why does the model ignore the water and look at the banks? Because ResNet18 was pre-trained on ImageNet. In natural photography (dogs, cars, people), object identity is defined by shapes and boundaries, not flat color fills. An edge detector is the most useful feature a CNN can learn.

⚠️
The Mechanistic Cause of River↔Highway Confusion
Because ResNet18 learned to detect objects via edges, it views a River as "two parallel edges" and a Highway as "two parallel edges". The visual Grad-CAM heatmaps for both classes look almost identical (a thick diagonal streak). This proves that the remaining 11 errors are not random failures — they are a direct consequence of the model's fundamental (and correct) reliance on edge-detection logic inherited from ImageNet.

Error Analysis — Hard Examples

Inspecting the exact images where ResNet18 failed to uncover label noise and semantic ambiguity.

🕵️ Investigating the River → Highway Errors

We wrote a script to extract the exact 11 validation images where the True label was `River` but ResNet18 predicted `Highway`. What we found completely validates the Grad-CAM edge-bias theory and reveals the massive challenge of "semantic ambiguity" in satellite imagery.

💡
The "Spurious Correlation" Trap
When examining the 11 misclassified images, we found they were NOT random mistakes. They fell into three highly logical categories that tricked the model's edge-detection neurons:
Visual Pattern in the RiverWhy the Model Saw a Highway
Boats or Objects in the WaterA white boat on a dark river looks identical to a white car on dark asphalt. The model learned that small white rectangles on dark paths = vehicles on a highway.
White Wakes / Water CurrentsA boat's wake or natural water rapids create stark white lines in the center of the dark river. To an edge detector, this perfectly mimics a white dashed lane divider on a highway.
Sharp Land-Water BoundariesExtremely straight, artificial canals or sharp river banks create perfect parallel lines. Without contextual clues, a straight grey canal looks exactly like a paved road.
🎯
Conclusion: We Have Hit the Semantic Noise Floor
The model is no longer making "stupid" mistakes. It is making highly logical mistakes based on visual evidence that is genuinely ambiguous even to a human (e.g. is this a canal or a road?). This proves that 97.7% Macro-F1 is likely the practical limit for this dataset without bringing in external geographic data or multispectral bands.

Model Comparison — EfficientNet-B0

Testing Compound Scaling against brute-force depth to find the best architecture for satellite imagery edge deployment.

⚙️ EfficientNet-B0 — Architecture & Experiment

After achieving 97.7% with ResNet18, we tested EfficientNet-B0 — a fundamentally different architectural philosophy. Instead of brute-force depth (ResNet stacks 18+ residual blocks), EfficientNet uses Compound Scaling: mathematically balancing Depth, Width, and Resolution simultaneously. It also includes Squeeze-and-Excitation (SE) blocks that act as a primitive attention mechanism — dynamically amplifying important feature channels and suppressing noise.

💡
Why EfficientNet Matters for Drone Deployment
EfficientNet-B0 has only 5.3 Million parameters vs ResNet18's 11.2 Million — less than half the size. On a battery-powered drone running a Jetson Nano or Raspberry Pi at the edge, this means significantly less memory, lower power draw, and faster real-time inference during flight.
